# The SPAR Group Limited (JSE: SPP) — Research Note

**Focus:** Management incentives · Risks for FY2026/FY2027 · Rand exposure · Capital allocation
**Prepared:** 5 June 2026 · **Analyst infrastructure:** JSE Equity Research (Cowork)
**Reporting currency:** ZAR · **Year-end:** late September (52/53-week retail calendar)
*For informational purposes only — not investment advice. Share-price and valuation data sourced via web search and may be delayed.*

---

## 1. Summary

SPAR is roughly two years into a self-inflicted turnaround. The disastrous offshore expansion (Poland, Switzerland) has been substantially unwound, leaving a simpler group — Southern Africa plus Ireland (BWG), with the UK (Appleby Westward) being sold during 2026. FY2025 (52 weeks to 26 September 2025) showed the *deleveraging* thesis working: net debt fell 40% to **R5.4bn** and Southern African operating profit rose **6.8%**. But the most recent data point — the **H1 FY2026 trading statement (29 May 2026)** — is a sharp negative: continuing-operations HEPS is guided **50–60% lower** year-on-year, driven by margin compression, a botched Black Friday and ongoing problems at the **KwaZulu-Natal (KZN) distribution centre**. The market has responded: the shares are down ~30% over the last 90 days to around **R64.60**.

The four themes you asked about are tightly linked. Management *incentives* are under live shareholder revolt (61% against the pay-implementation report) just as the *leadership* has turned over completely (new CEO, new CFO since 1 March 2026). *Rand exposure* has narrowed to essentially one foreign asset (euro-denominated Ireland) but carries a legacy ZAR-denominated debt drag from the offshore exits. And *capital allocation* is the whole equity story: SPAR has paid **no dividend** for two years while it de-gears, with the promise — not yet delivered — of restored shareholder returns "in the short to medium term." The 10 June 2026 interim results (and any dividend signal) are the next catalyst.

## 3. Management incentives

**The structure.** Executive pay comprises a guaranteed package, an annual short-term incentive (STI/cash bonus) and a long-term incentive (LTI) of performance shares vesting over several years. Per the company, awards are conditioned on a mix of **headline earnings, return on capital employed (ROCE), cash flow and long-term shareholder returns**, with payouts scaled down when targets are missed and capped on the upside.

**The revolt.** At the 2025 AGM, **61% of shareholders voted against** the (non-binding, advisory) resolution on the *implementation* of executive pay; the remuneration *policy* itself passed but still drew **>30% dissent**. The trigger was a **R9.5m lump-sum payment to the former CFO, Mark Godfrey** (retired 31 December 2024), which the IoDSA noted the policy did not appear to provide for and which was not clearly explained in the remuneration report — even though no performance bonus, not even pro-rated, was paid to him. SPAR has since invited dissenting shareholders to a board engagement.

**Reported pay (FY2025).** Outgoing CEO **Angelo Swartz**'s total package was reported at **R18.192m** (News24) — ~169x the lowest-paid employee — though IOL cites R16m for "basic salary and other contributions" (the gap is scope: total reward vs. fixed-plus-benefits; reconcile against the remuneration report). New CFO-then-CEO **Reeza Isaacs** earned **R12.7m**.

**Why it matters for 2026/2027.** Two things to watch:
1. **Alignment vs. the turnaround.** With HEPS, ROCE and cash flow as the metrics, FY2026's collapse in continuing HEPS (guided -50% to -60% at the half) should mechanically suppress STI/LTI vesting — *if* the committee holds the line. A second year of high "against" votes, or discretionary awards that override the formula, would be a governance red flag.
2. **The advisory vote is non-binding** under SA/King IV. The board can implement despite a sub-75% vote, but repeated dissent carries reputational and sustainability risk. This is a live tension between a refreshed management team that needs retention incentives and a shareholder base that has lost patience.

**Leadership turnover (a related incentive/risk).** The entire top of the house changed on **1 March 2026**: Swartz resigned (effective 28 February, after 19 years and ~2.5 years as CEO); CFO **Reeza Isaacs** (ex-Woolworths Holdings finance director) stepped up to **Group CEO**; **Megan Pydigadu** joined as **Group CFO**. Below board level, the H1 update flags a "refreshed" executive team — new Group CMO, a dedicated MD for SA Groceries & Liquor, and leadership changes at KZN. New incentive arrangements for this team will need re-approval and will be scrutinised.

## 4. Rand exposure

SPAR reports in ZAR. The offshore restructuring has **dramatically simplified** the currency profile:

- **Poland (PLN)** — exited (the original misadventure); residual term debt was refinanced into a **ZAR bridge facility (~R1.18bn)** sitting in Southern Africa.
- **Switzerland (CHF)** — sold; SPAR effectively **paid ~R683m to exit** a negative-value business.
- **UK / Appleby Westward (GBP)** — asset-purchase agreement signed 18 May 2026 with A.F. Blakemore; completion staged June–September 2026. Classified as discontinued (IFRS 5).
- **Ireland / BWG (EUR)** — **retained**; now the group's principal foreign exposure at **~€1.74bn of revenue** and a ~3.3% operating margin.
- **Sri Lanka (LKR)** — small JV; immaterial to the group but currency-volatile.

**Net read:** post-restructuring, translation risk is concentrated in the **euro (Ireland)**. The H1 FY2026 update illustrates the sensitivity neatly — Ireland grew **+3.4% in EUR but +6.1% in ZAR**, i.e. rand weakness flattered the reported number by ~270bps. Ireland is a genuine **rand hedge** within the portfolio: hard-currency cash flows, leverage of 1.71x and interest cover of 11.19x, a stable food-and-foodservice franchise.

**The sting in the tail is a ZAR liability, not an FX asset.** The legacy offshore debt assumed onto the SA balance sheet generated **non-deductible interest** and pushed up the effective tax rate — a direct earnings and tax drag carried in rands. Unwinding this ("simplify the capital structure to reduce interest drag, normalise the tax burden") is an explicit FY2026/27 management objective. Watch the effective tax rate normalise as the bridge facility is repaid.

## 5. Capital allocation

Capital allocation **is** the SPAR equity story right now, and it runs in a clear sequence: *exit offshore → de-gear → restore returns.*

**De-gearing (the priority).** Net debt fell **40% to R5.4bn** in FY2025 (from R9.1bn), with group leverage down to **1.74x** (from 2.41x). Southern Africa gearing improved to 1.75x with interest cover of 4.50x; Ireland sits at 1.71x / 11.19x cover. Cash generated from operations rose **13.3% to R5.45bn**. The Switzerland and Poland disposals plus working-capital discipline drove most of the improvement. **Caveat:** at H1 FY2026, net debt rose again on seasonal working-capital build and Easter timing — management expects it to unwind into H2; **all banking covenants were met**.

**Dividends — still withheld.** The board declared **no dividend for FY2025** (nil in FY2024 too), judging continued de-gearing the better use of cash. It has committed to **reinstating shareholder returns "in the short to medium term," via dividends or buybacks**, reassessed regularly. Whether a token interim or a clear timetable appears at the **10 June 2026** results is the key near-term catalyst — though the weak H1 makes a payout less likely.

**Capex and bolt-ons.**
- **SAP rollout** — a "risk-controlled" continuation of the ERP programme whose earlier KZN implementation caused the original supply-chain damage. A capex and operational-risk item through FY2026/27.
- **Pet Storey** — acquisition of Pet Masters (12 stores converted by Nov 2025); a small growth adjacency, alongside SPAR Health (Scriptwise/pharmacy) and Build it.
- **Living wage** — voluntary cost commitment: R12,500/month floor from January 2026, rising to **R15,000/month from January 2027** (cost-to-company) — a deliberate, recurring cost-base increase.

**The bottom line on capital:** balance-sheet repair is real and ahead of schedule, but the *return of capital* — the part shareholders are waiting for — has not yet started, and FY2026's earnings setback pushes it further out.

## 6. Risks for FY2026 / FY2027

| Risk | Why it matters | Status / watch |
|---|---|---|
| **KZN distribution centre** | Root-cause: insufficient logistics-capacity planning; hit service levels, costs and margin. Still the single biggest operational risk, monitored at board level. | 3 consecutive profitable months (Feb–Apr 2026), but below target; new KZN leadership. |
| **Margin compression (SA)** | SA gross margin down 20–40bps at H1 on Black Friday promo subsidy + KZN; above-inflation costs and rising debtor impairments. | Cost-realignment programme; new CMO tightening promo discipline. Benefits skew to H2 FY2026 / FY2027. |
| **Earnings quality / impairments** | Repeated "extraordinary items" and a more conservative debtor-provisioning methodology cloud the underlying run-rate. | ~R128m impairments at H1 (vs R71m); further year-end provisioning flagged. |
| **Debtor / credit risk** | SPAR finances independent retailers; overdue balances rose even as debtors' days held at 32. A retailer-solvency cycle would bite. | "Key area of focus"; expect further provisions. |
| **Independent-retailer loyalty** | The franchise model depends on voluntary retailer loyalty (78.6%, slipping from 79.2%); KZN loyalty below target. Guild discontent is an existential, SPAR-specific risk. | Five named retailer issues being addressed; trading-terms renegotiation. |
| **SAP / ERP rollout** | The system that caused the original KZN crisis is still being rolled out. Execution risk to supply chain and franchise continuity. | "Risk-controlled" phased approach. |
| **Leadership transition** | Entire C-suite new since March 2026; first-time group CEO. Execution and continuity risk during a turnaround. | Predecessor available 3 months for handover. |
| **Governance / remuneration** | 61% against pay implementation; non-binding but reputationally live. | Board engagement underway. |
| **Macro / consumer** | SA revenue growth below internal selling-price inflation, itself below food CPI — i.e. real volume loss and competitive pressure. Rising fuel cost lifts logistics/distribution cost. | Management explicitly cautious on H2 FY2026. |
| **Disposal execution (AWG/UK)** | Staged completion Jun–Sep 2026; price/terms and any residual liabilities. | Agreement signed (A.F. Blakemore), 18 May 2026. |
| **Dividend disappointment** | Two years of nil dividends; the re-rating thesis depends on returns resuming. A further deferral at the 10 June results is a sentiment risk. | No FY2025 dividend; "short to medium term" language only. |

## 7. Questions for management (10 June 2026 results)

1. **KZN:** What is the structural fix beyond leadership changes — is the gross-margin gap to peer DCs a function of cost-to-serve, mix, or pricing, and when do you expect KZN at target margin? Has the SAP rollout schedule changed as a result?
2. **Capital returns:** Given covenant headroom at 1.74x, what is the explicit net-debt or leverage threshold that triggers a resumed dividend or buyback, and what is the realistic timetable now that H1 earnings have stepped down?
3. **Earnings quality:** How much of the H1 HEPS decline is "clean" trading vs. one-offs (Black Friday, conservative provisioning, legacy impairments)? What is the normalised continuing-ops run-rate into FY2027?
4. **Legacy debt & tax:** How much non-deductible legacy (Poland) interest remains in the SA entity, and what effective tax rate should we model for FY2027 once the bridge facility is repaid?
5. **Remuneration:** After 61% dissent, what specific changes to the pay framework (and to exit-payment policy) will the committee make, and how will FY2026's earnings collapse flow through STI/LTI vesting?
